## The Core Mismatch

Most commerce sites are built around a browser session that behaves like a human shopper in four specific ways:

1. It can keep cookies and local state intact across redirects.
2. It can complete multi-step flows without losing context.
3. It can solve or bypass human verification steps when prompted.
4. It can interpret UI-only state changes, like disabled buttons, modal dialogs, and transient error banners.

Agents often fail not because they cannot “understand” the store, but because commerce logic is spread across places that are hard to automate reliably: client-side state, third-party redirects, anti-bot checks, and identity verification.

That is why the most fragile parts of e-commerce are usually not browsing or product selection. They are the handoff points: login, shipping, payment, and post-purchase confirmation.

## What Agents Can Solve Today

Agents can already handle a meaningful subset of commerce tasks when the site is reasonably structured.

In practice, they do best on flows like:

- adding known SKUs to cart
- comparing product pages with stable markup
- filling shipping and billing forms
- completing checkout on sites with server-rendered steps
- retrying after temporary network or validation errors
- using saved credentials in a persistent browser profile

They struggle much more when the site depends on fragile client-side behavior, such as:

- cart state stored only in JavaScript memory
- checkout buttons that appear only after hidden validation passes
- address verification that depends on UI timing
- anti-bot challenges inserted mid-flow
- MFA or phone confirmation triggered after payment submission

So the practical limit is not “can an agent browse?” It is “does the site expose enough of the transaction in a stable, machine-executable way?”

## A More Realistic View of “Agent-Friendly”

“Agent-friendly” should not mean “remove all friction.”

A better definition is: the site exposes a clear, machine-readable path for routine transactions, while reserving extra verification for genuinely risky cases.

For example:

- a repeat buyer could use a delegated token instead of re-entering identity details
- a low-value order could skip phone verification entirely
- a high-risk purchase could trigger step-up checks only at the final step
- cart, shipping, and payment state could be available through stable endpoints instead of hidden UI state

That distinction matters because not all commerce needs the same level of scrutiny. A $12 reorder should not be forced through the same flow as a regulated or abuse-prone transaction.

In other words, agent-friendly commerce is not about making the site easier in general. It is about making the right parts programmable and the necessary friction explicit.
